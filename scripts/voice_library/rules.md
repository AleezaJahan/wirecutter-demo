# Voice Rules

These rules are extracted from patterns observed across dozens of Wirecutter articles.
They define what the writing sounds like at a structural and vocabulary level.

---

## Banned words and phrases

Never use these. They are hallmarks of AI-generated or marketing copy:

- "boasts" (as in "boasts impressive features")
- "elevate" / "elevate your experience"
- "seamless" / "seamlessly"
- "game-changer" / "game-changing"
- "landscape" (as in "the headphone landscape")
- "cutting-edge"
- "innovative" / "innovation"
- "dive into" / "dive deeper" / "deep dive"
- "plethora"
- "comprehensive"
- "robust"
- "leverage" (as a verb)
- "revolutionize" / "revolutionary"
- "unparalleled"
- "best-in-class"
- "holistic"
- "synergy"
- "paradigm"
- "state-of-the-art"
- "world-class"
- "top-notch" (acceptable once per guide, Wirecutter uses it sparingly)
- "a must-have"
- "In the world of..."
- "When it comes to..."
- "If you're in the market for..."
- "Look no further"
- "Without further ado"
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "In today's market..."
- "Whether you're a beginner or a pro..."
- "navigate" / "navigating" (when used metaphorically, e.g. "navigate the landscape")
- "ensure" (use "make sure" instead)
- "utilize" (use "use" instead)
- "moreover" / "furthermore" / "additionally"
- "notably"
- "streamlined"
- "offers a range of"
- "designed to"
- "stands out as"
- "making it ideal for"
- "perfect for those who"

## Banned punctuation and formatting

- **Em dashes (—) are BANNED.** Never use them. Use commas, periods, or parentheses instead. This is non-negotiable. Em dashes are the single biggest AI tell in generated prose.
- **Semicolons:** Use sparingly (max 1 per paragraph). Prefer two short sentences.
- **Exclamation marks:** Never in body copy. They make the writing sound like marketing.
- **Ellipses (...):** Never. They create a vague, trailing tone.
- **Colon lists in prose:** Avoid "It excels in three areas: speed, power, and efficiency." Just write it as a sentence.

---

## Structural rules

1. **Sentence length:** Average 15-20 words. Mix short punchy sentences (5-8 words) with longer ones. Never exceed 35 words in a single sentence.

2. **Paragraph length:** Max 3-4 sentences per paragraph. Break up longer paragraphs.

3. **Contractions always.** "It's", "don't", "won't", "can't", "we've", "you'll". Never "it is", "do not", "will not" in body copy.

4. **Second person.** "You", "your", not "one", "one's", "users", "consumers".

5. **Active voice.** "We tested 70 purifiers" not "70 purifiers were tested by our team".

6. **No stacking adjectives.** Max 2 adjectives before a noun. "A quiet, efficient purifier" is fine. "An innovative, cutting-edge, high-performance, versatile purifier" is not.

7. **Lead each section/paragraph with a claim.** "It's an excellent cleaner." Then support it. Never lead with "The product features..." or "One of the key aspects of..."

8. **Vary sentence openings.** No two consecutive sentences should start with the same word or phrase. Especially avoid starting multiple sentences with "The [product]..." or "It...".

---

## Content rules

1. **Every pick needs at least one specific flaw.** Not vague: "some limitations". Specific: "only one side brush, so dust pickup near baseboards can be an issue."

2. **Claims require evidence.** "Excellent cleaner" needs "picked up 99.8% of smoke in 30 minutes" or "snagged all the Cheerios around barstool legs." If you don't have test data, cite the reviewer source.

3. **Compare to alternatives.** "Not as nuanced as the Moccamaster", "within an ounce of measured dust pickup from our top pick". Position products relative to each other.

4. **Price context.** Always frame price relative to what you get. "Despite costing four times as much", "all for less than $100".

5. **Practical details > spec sheets.** "Quiet enough to sleep near" > "48 dB noise level". Lead with what it means for the reader, then give the number.

6. **No generic praise.** Never: "This is a great option for anyone looking for quality." Always: what specifically is great, for whom, measured how.

7. **Acknowledge uncertainty.** "Probably", "in our experience", "seems to", "we expect". Wirecutter doesn't claim omniscience.

## Tone calibration

On a 1-5 scale:

- **Funny ↔ Serious:** 2 (mostly serious, occasional dry wit when earned by the situation)
- **Formal ↔ Casual:** 3.5 (conversational but competent, like a knowledgeable friend, not a social media post)
- **Respectful ↔ Irreverent:** 4 (respectful of reader's time and money, but willing to be blunt about bad products)
- **Enthusiastic ↔ Matter-of-fact:** 3.5 (genuinely enthusiastic about good products, but never hype-y)
- **Salesy ↔ Advisory:** 5 (purely advisory. Wirecutter never hard-sells)

## Canada Picks adaptations

These adaptations apply on top of the Wirecutter voice:

1. **Canadian context is factual, not patriotic.** "Headquartered in Toronto" not "proudly Canadian". "Available at Amazon.ca" not "available right here in Canada!".

2. **Price is always in CAD.** Always say "CAD" or "$X CAD" on first reference. After that, "$X" is fine.

3. **Reviewer sources are named.** "Recommended by RTINGS and Vacuum Wars". The reader should know who we're aggregating.

4. **Availability is a first-class concern.** "In stock at Amazon.ca", "ships within Canada", "not available from Canadian retailers". Always surface this.

## Aggregator voice (critical, read this carefully)

We are NOT Wirecutter. We do not test products ourselves. We aggregate expert
reviews and verify Canadian availability. This changes how we can write, but it
should NOT make the voice passive or detached. The goal: sound like a sharp editor
who's read everything and is giving you the bottom line.

### What we CANNOT say

- "In our tests..." / "We tested..." / "When we tried..."
- "We measured..." / "We found in our testing..."
- "In our hands..." / "After weeks of use..."
- Any claim that implies we physically used the product

### What we CAN say (and should)

**Attribution to specific reviewers:**
- "RTINGS measured its noise cancellation at 26.2 dB, among the best they've tested."
- "Vacuum Wars ranked it first for pet hair pickup."
- "Consumer Reports gave it their highest score for cleaning performance."

**Aggregation language that still has authority:**
- "Across three independent reviews, this model consistently scored highest for suction power."
- "Every reviewer we checked flagged the same flaw: the app is clunky."
- "It's the most-recommended robot vacuum among the sources we trust."
- "Three of four reviewers named it their top pick."

**Our own editorial judgment (what we add):**
- "We chose it because..." (editorial decision based on the data)
- "We think this is the right pick for most Canadians because..."
- "The reviewer consensus is clear: ..."
- "What stood out across sources was..."
- "The main knock against it, across every review we read, is..."

**Canadian verification (this IS our first-hand work):**
- "We verified it's in stock at Amazon.ca for $349 CAD."
- "We confirmed this ships within Canada."
- "We couldn't find a Canadian retailer carrying this model."

### Phrasing swaps (Wirecutter → Canada Picks)

| Wirecutter says | We say instead |
|---|---|
| "In our tests, it picked up 99.8% of smoke" | "RTINGS measured 99.8% smoke reduction in their controlled tests" |
| "We've tested more than 70 purifiers" | "We cross-referenced reviews from RTINGS, Consumer Reports, and Wirecutter, covering more than 70 models combined" |
| "It was the most thorough cleaner we tested" | "It scored highest for cleaning thoroughness across three independent reviews" |
| "We found the app clunky" | "Multiple reviewers flagged the app as clunky, and owner reviews echo that" |
| "We recommend the Roborock Q7 M5+" | "The Roborock Q7 M5+ is our pick" (we CAN recommend, that's editorial judgment) |
| "After weeks of testing..." | "Based on long-term testing by RTINGS and Vacuum Wars..." |
| "In our listening tests..." | "In RTINGS' standardized listening tests..." |

### The key principle

We still make confident recommendations. "This is our pick" is fine. We earned
that through research and verification. What we don't do is pretend we held the
product. The authority comes from:
1. Breadth of sources ("we cross-referenced 4 independent reviewers")
2. Consistency of signal ("every source agreed on this")
3. Our own Canadian verification work ("we confirmed availability and pricing")
4. Editorial judgment ("given the Canadian market, we think...")
