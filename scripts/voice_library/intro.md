# Guide Intro Examples

These are real Wirecutter intro paragraphs. Study the pattern: open with a concrete
claim about the category, set realistic expectations, name specific picks by the end.
Never open with a generic "In the world of..." or "If you're looking for...".

---

## Example 1: Robot Vacuums

Robot vacuums are feats of engineering. Even a basic bot can keep your floors tidy with little effort on your part, handling pet hair and dust adeptly. Top-tier models can map your home, schedule cleanings, take voice commands, and empty themselves.

But don't set your expectations too high. These machines excel in routine midweek cleanups but can't replace traditional plug-in vacuums, especially on rugs. And even the fanciest models can drive you mad, getting tangled or trapped, or missing piles of dirt.

We've tested a range of models over the years, from cheap, aimless bumblers to sophisticated machines that (usually) navigate with ease.

---

## Example 2: Air Purifiers

Air purifiers are fundamentally simple machines — little more than a fan and a filter. But a well-designed purifier can capture virtually all airborne allergens, bacteria, viruses, and smoke, and do it quietly and efficiently, around the clock, for years on end.

---

## Example 3: Noise-Cancelling Headphones

For frequent flyers or commuters on public transit, reducing the noise around you is the difference between enduring a trip and enjoying it. Immersing yourself in the hush created by a great pair of noise-cancelling headphones, combined with the opening chords of your favorite song, can make your muscles relax faster than slipping under a weighted blanket. And they're easier to pack, too.

---

## Example 4: Drip Coffee Makers

Although a cheap drip coffee maker can fulfill your basic need for a big, hot pot of coffee first thing in the morning, only a great machine will ensure that your pot consistently tastes delicious.

---

## Voice notes

- **Open with a specific truth about the product category** (what it does, what it's for, why it matters). Not a definition, not a history lesson.
- **Immediately set expectations** (what the product can and can't do). Wirecutter never oversells.
- **Keep it short.** 2-3 paragraphs max. The intro is a doorway, not the article.
- **Contractions always.** "Don't", "can't", "won't", "it's".
- **Second person.** "Your floors", "your home", not "one's home".
- **Concrete details over abstractions.** "Pet hair and dust" not "various debris types". "Getting tangled or trapped" not "encountering navigation difficulties".

---

## How to adapt for Canada Picks (aggregator voice)

The intro pattern stays the same. Open with a truth about the category, set
expectations. But swap the Wirecutter "we tested" framing for aggregation framing.

**Wirecutter writes:**
"We've tested a range of models over the years, from cheap, aimless bumblers to sophisticated machines that (usually) navigate with ease."

**Canada Picks equivalent:**
"We cross-referenced recommendations from RTINGS, Vacuum Wars, and Consumer Reports, then verified every pick ships to Canada with CAD pricing."

**Wirecutter writes:**
"We've tested more than 70 air purifiers over the past decade, and the Coway Airmega Mighty2 is our top pick among them."

**Canada Picks equivalent:**
"After comparing picks from RTINGS, Wirecutter, and Consumer Reports (covering more than 70 models), we think the Coway Airmega Mighty2 is the best air purifier you can buy in Canada right now."

The intro should still end with a confident recommendation. "We think X is your best bet" is fine. That's editorial judgment, not a testing claim.
