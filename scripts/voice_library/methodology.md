# "How We Picked" / Methodology Section Examples

These sections explain criteria and testing methods. They're specific about what
was measured and how, without being academic. They read like a knowledgeable friend
explaining their process, not a lab report.

---

## Example 1: Robot Vacuums

**Testing criteria (how they evaluated):**

Cleaning power: We measured and compared how much debris each model picked up.

Intelligence: We checked how well each model performed around obstacles and assessed the user-friendliness of app-controlled bots.

Convenience: We checked each bot's noise level, whether it moved easily under and around furniture, and whether its brushes could tackle corners.

Sustainability: We checked whether a model could be repaired and whether replacement parts are easily available.

**Testing detail (what they actually did):**

For every robot vacuum we tested, we ran at least three regular cleaning cycles across our test space and home environments. We evenly spread a mix of rice, cat litter, and Cheerios around the living room, hallway, bathroom, and kitchen areas with furniture, obstacles, thresholds, and a couple of low-to-medium-pile area rugs. We weighed each bot's dustbin before and after the cleaning cycle to determine how much debris it had collected.

## Example 2: Air Purifiers

**Criteria (what mattered):**

We confirm it can move enough air. When considering a new purifier, we first refer to its airflow, usually given as its clean air delivery rate, or CADR, in cubic feet per minute.

We want true HEPA filters. HEPA's a prerequisite.

We emphasize livability factors. Purifiers tend to live where people work, relax, and sleep, so convenience factors are important. We look for quiet operation, displays that can shut off, simple controls, compact size and decent looks.

We consider value. Without setting a strict price limit, we compare cost against specs, upkeep and electricity costs over the course of five years of operation, ratings from owners, and manufacturers' histories of reliability and customer service.

**Testing detail:**

At the start of each test, we take a baseline reading of the ambient air quality for three minutes. Next, we burn five wooden matches and let the smoke circulate for two minutes to raise the particulate levels in the air. We then run the purifiers for 30 minutes.

Smoke particles are right in the 0.3-micron size range that's the hardest size to capture. If a purifier does well on smoke, it will do even better on larger particles like dust and pollen.

## Example 3: Drip Coffee Makers

We focused primarily on machines certified by the Specialty Coffee Association (SCA) to meet its standards. Focusing on SCA-certified machines allowed us to be fairly certain that the models we tested met these baseline criteria, which are crucial to brewing good coffee:

- Brews with water around 200 °F: This helps to extract the best flavor from coffee. Cooler water results in a weak, flat-tasting brew, while boiling water can bring out unpleasant flavors.
- Brews evenly: We looked for models that saturate all the grounds in the brew basket with hot water in the first minute, to produce a balanced, full-bodied pot of coffee every time.

Beyond those brewing fundamentals, the things that separate a good coffee maker from a great one are all about how it feels to use:
- Brews quickly: We chose models that produce a full pot in under eight minutes.
- Clear controls: These make it possible for a groggy person or a houseguest to brew a pot of coffee without confusion.

## Voice notes

- **Lead with what matters, not how you tested.** Criteria first, process second.
- **Use plain language for technical concepts.** "Little more than a fan and a filter", "the hardest size to capture", "a groggy person or a houseguest".
- **Concrete measurements are good.** "Five wooden matches", "30 minutes", "under eight minutes", "200 °F". Numbers build trust.
- **Explain why each criterion matters to the reader.** Not just "we measured noise" but "purifiers live where people work, relax, and sleep, so convenience factors are important."
- **Keep the tone of a competent friend**, not a reviewer who needs to sound impressive.

## How to adapt for Canada Picks (aggregator voice)

We don't have a test lab. Our "how we picked" is about our process: which sources
we trust, how we score products, and how we verify Canadian availability. This is
actually interesting to readers. It's transparent and different from Wirecutter.

**Canada Picks methodology template:**

We started with recommendations from [source names], independent reviewers who
do hands-on, long-term testing. We looked at which products showed up across
multiple sources and how consistently they were recommended.

From there, we scored each product based on reviewer consensus: products recommended
by more sources ranked higher. We also factored in price tiers (separating budget,
mid-range, and upgrade picks) so you're not comparing a $50 product against a $500 one.

Then we verified every product for Canada. We checked whether it's sold by a
Canadian retailer, confirmed the price in CAD, and flagged whether it's in stock.
Products that aren't available in Canada were filtered out, regardless of how
highly reviewers rated them.

Finally, we looked for Canadian-owned alternatives. If a Canadian company makes
a competitive product in the category, we surface it, but only if it meets a
minimum quality bar.

**Key differences from Wirecutter:**
- Wirecutter explains what they measured. We explain which experts we trust and why.
- Wirecutter cites their own test results. We cite the consensus across sources.
- We add a step Wirecutter doesn't have: Canadian verification. Lean into this. It's our unique value.
