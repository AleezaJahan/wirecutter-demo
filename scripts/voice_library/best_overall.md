# "Best Overall" / Top Pick Write-Up Examples

These are per-product write-ups for the top pick. Study the pattern: a one-line
headline that's a claim (not just the product name), then 2-4 paragraphs that each
start with a bold assertion backed by specific evidence.

---

## Example 1: Roborock Q7 M5+ (Robot Vacuum — Top Pick)

**Headline:** A smart, quick robot that empties itself

**Summary blurb:** This bot is a top-notch cleaner and a great navigator that avoids (most) obstacles while keeping on top of scattered debris. It comes with a charging dock that sucks debris from its dustbin after it cleans.

**Write-up:**

A robot vacuum can be a handy sidekick to a plug-in or cordless stick vacuum, able to handle day-to-day maintenance cleanings. The Roborock Q7 M5+ is a thorough and quiet cleaner with smooth navigation and easily customizable settings. This model also comes with a compact self-emptying dock that automatically empties its dustbin into a bag.

It's an excellent cleaner. Between its strong suction power, fearless approach to corners and tight spots under and around furniture, and efficient cleaning patterns, the Q7 M5+ is an extremely thorough cleaner.

In our tests, it picked up Cheerios, birdseed, fur, and dust off of hard floors as well as carpets at a first-rate level. It snagged all the Cheerios we placed around the legs of a barstool — a task that foiled a number of other robots we tried — and it missed fewer spots under furniture and near edges than bots that cost double and triple as much.

It's an efficient and brave navigator. Using a combination of bump sensors and a lidar laser rangefinder, the Q7 M5+ quickly maps your spaces, identifies problem areas, and efficiently navigates with relatively few issues.

No robot vacuum is immune to snags and tangles, but the Q7 M5+ ably avoids problem areas like loose cords or carpet tassels, and it rarely required human assistance to complete a run in our tests.

**Flaws but not dealbreakers:**

It misses spots. Most robot vacuums are prone to missing debris in certain situations, and the Q7 M5+ is no exception. When it was between surfaces — say, one wheel on carpet and another on hard floor — it missed some debris.

Also, it has only one side brush, which means dust pickup near baseboards and edges can be an issue.

## Example 2: Coway Airmega Mighty2 (Air Purifier — Top Pick)

**Headline:** Exceptional, efficient, easy to live with

**Summary blurb:** The Coway Airmega Mighty2 is one of the best-performing purifiers we've ever tested, and in terms of liveability, it's the most well-thought-out. It's quieter, more energy efficient, and easier to adjust and maintain than the original Mighty it's based on.

**Write-up:**

The Coway Airmega Mighty2 is among the most exceptional air purifiers of the 70-plus we've tested in more than a decade of publishing this guide. Here's what sets it apart.

Performance-wise, it's among the most effective purifiers we've tested. Only a handful of purifiers we've tested have exceeded the Mighty2's smoke reduction rate: 99.8% in half an hour on its highest of four fan speeds. The Mighty2 also reduced the smoke by 99.1% on its second-highest fan speed — whose noise output falls below our 50-decibel limit. This combination of rapid air cleaning and tolerable noise levels is almost unheard of in such a physically compact machine.

It's a special kind of quiet. The Mighty2's sound is notably flat and stable, pleasantly reminiscent of cabin noise.

It's engineered for energy efficiency. We measured the Mighty2's power draw at 48.4 watts on its highest fan setting. The original Mighty draws 68.7 on its highest speed — nearly 42% more — even though the machines are moving virtually identical amounts of air.

**Flaws but not dealbreakers:**

The control panel is less tactile than we'd like. The touchscreen control panel, which has no physical indications of where each icon is located, may be difficult for people with visual or dexterity impairments to use.

## Example 3: Sony WH-1000XM6 (Headphones — Top Pick)

**Headline:** The best wireless noise-cancelling headphones

**Summary blurb:** This lightweight, comfortable pair of headphones provides an excellent combination of active and passive noise reduction, as well as great sound quality. But it comes at a high price.

**Write-up:**

The Sony WH-1000XM6 headphones offer an excellent combination of noise reduction, comfort, and sound quality in a lightweight design that's great for travel.

Although the WH-1000XM6's active noise cancellation is a few decibels shy of the best reduction we've measured in the airplane band, it's still up there among the top performers. And this pair's passive isolation allows it to block out high-frequency sounds better than any headphone pair we've tested to date. The result is a pleasingly quiet experience, whether you're in an airplane, train, or busy office.

Right out of the box, it sounds incredible — among the best we've tested. Most people are likely to be thrilled by the clarity of the highs, which were not piercing or harsh in our tests. Bass notes sounded pure, not smeared or boomy.

The six microphones delivered clear vocals for calls when we tested them in an office setting. Voices sounded natural, and the background-noise-reducing software easily handled office chatter, a running sink, or a coffee grinder. But when our surroundings got busy, voices could sound compressed.

**Flaws but not dealbreakers:**

The ANC is not adjustable. We wish Sony provided the option to reduce the intensity of the noise cancellation sometimes, which can be especially helpful for people who experience eardrum suck.

We also wish that Sony's headphone app were more user-friendly. The app hasn't evolved much over the years, and it's unnecessarily busy and complicated.

## Example 4: OXO Brew 9-Cup (Coffee Maker — Top Pick)

**Headline:** Good coffee, great features

**Summary blurb:** This brewer not only makes a tasty cup of coffee, but it's also the only one of our picks to feature an auto-brew function that can be programmed to have a pot ready when you wake up.

**Write-up:**

Stylish from top to bottom, though tall and difficult to fit under some kitchen cabinets, the OXO Brew 9-Cup Coffee Maker comes with a number of convenient features. The auto-brew function is the most important of those features: It lets you program the brewer to make coffee before you wake up.

It makes good coffee, quickly. Coffee brewed in this machine is pleasant, though not as nuanced compared with the Moccamaster. And the OXO 9-Cup brewed a full pot in just under 8 minutes.

It has many helpful automated functions. Every brew starts with automatic pre-infusion (aka blooming) which briefly wets the coffee grounds before brewing to prepare them for better extraction.

The carafe does a good job of retaining heat. Although the coffee started out cooler than brews from the Moccamaster KBT, it was hotter after four hours sitting in the carafe.

**Flaws but not dealbreakers:**

In general, the controls aren't immediately intuitive. You have to press the button or start twisting the dial to switch from the digital clock to the brewing options.

The carafe has a narrow opening, so you need a bottle brush to clean inside.

## Voice notes

- **Headline is a claim, not the product name.** "A smart, quick robot that empties itself" not "Roborock Q7 M5+ Review". "Good coffee, great features" not "Best Drip Coffee Maker".
- **Each paragraph leads with a bold statement, then backs it up.** "It's an excellent cleaner. Between its strong suction power..." / "It's a special kind of quiet. The Mighty2's sound is notably flat and stable..."
- **Specific test results build trust.** "99.8% in half an hour", "picked up Cheerios, birdseed, fur, and dust", "brewed a full pot in just under 8 minutes".
- **Always include flaws.** Every pick has a "Flaws but not dealbreakers" section. This is non-negotiable. The flaws are specific, not vague ("only one side brush" not "some cleaning limitations").
- **Compare to alternatives naturally.** "Not as nuanced compared with the Moccamaster", "missed fewer spots than bots that cost double and triple as much".
- **No generic praise.** Never "This is a great product for anyone looking for quality." Always specific: what exactly is great, measured how.

## How to adapt for Canada Picks (aggregator voice)

The structure stays identical: headline → blurb → paragraphs with bold claims → flaws.
The difference is attribution.

**Wirecutter writes:**
"It's an excellent cleaner. Between its strong suction power, fearless approach to corners and tight spots under and around furniture, and efficient cleaning patterns, the Q7 M5+ is an extremely thorough cleaner. In our tests, it picked up Cheerios, birdseed, fur, and dust off of hard floors as well as carpets at a first-rate level."

**Canada Picks equivalent:**
"It's a standout cleaner. RTINGS rated its suction power among the highest they've measured, and Vacuum Wars ranked it first for bare-floor pickup. Across every review we checked, it consistently scored at or near the top for thoroughness on both hard floors and carpet."

**Key moves:**
- Keep the bold opening claim: "It's a standout cleaner."
- Replace "in our tests" with specific reviewer attribution: "RTINGS rated...", "Vacuum Wars ranked..."
- Use aggregation to strengthen: "Across every review we checked" is actually more authoritative than a single test, when the consensus is clear.
- For flaws, same approach: "Multiple reviewers noted..." or "The main complaint across reviews is..."
